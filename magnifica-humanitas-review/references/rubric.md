# Magnifica Humanitas Rubric Reference

Use this reference when filling `assets/report-template.md`.

## Scoring scale

- `0`: Harms, contradicts, or ignores the principle.
- `1`: Names the principle but offers weak or mostly symbolic evidence.
- `2`: Partly satisfies the principle with meaningful but incomplete practices.
- `3`: Strongly satisfies the principle with concrete, accountable evidence.

## Summary test

Ask: is this building Babel or rebuilding Jerusalem?

- Babel pattern: concentration of power, domination, opacity, uniformity, efficiency over dignity, profit over the weak, technical inevitability, and a single imposed language of data or control.
- Jerusalem pattern: shared responsibility, dignity, dialogue, justice, truth, subsidiarity, solidarity, care for the vulnerable, and the human person at the center.

## Metrics

| # | Criterion | Evaluation question |
|---:|---|---|
| 1 | Person-centered purpose | Does the thing serve real human flourishing, or mainly efficiency, profit, control, novelty, or prestige? |
| 2 | Irreplaceability of the human person | Does it preserve human judgment, conscience, relationships, embodiment, and responsibility where they matter? |
| 3 | Respect for limits and vulnerability | Does it care for weakness, dependency, disability, age, poverty, illness, and embodied life rather than treating them as defects to optimize away? |
| 4 | Common good orientation | Are benefits shared broadly, or concentrated among owners, elites, early adopters, or powerful institutions? |
| 5 | Preferential care for the vulnerable | Does it improve conditions for the poor, marginalized, excluded, displaced, or digitally powerless? |
| 6 | Universal access and fair distribution | Are access, affordability, language, disability, geography, and digital literacy actively considered? |
| 7 | Truthfulness | Does it promote accuracy, explain uncertainty, resist manipulation, and avoid deceptive presentation? |
| 8 | Epistemic health | Does it strengthen people's ability to think, deliberate, learn, and judge, rather than making them passive or dependent? |
| 9 | Healthy public discourse | Does it reduce polarization, humiliation, outrage, propaganda, and algorithmic distortion? |
| 10 | Human agency | Does it expand meaningful human choice, or does it steer, addict, coerce, surveil, or manipulate? |
| 11 | Transparency and explainability | Can affected people understand how decisions are made and why outcomes occur? |
| 12 | Accountability | Are there clear owners, appeal paths, audit trails, and consequences when harm occurs? |
| 13 | Dignity of work | Does it respect workers, protect livelihoods, improve working conditions, and avoid treating labor as disposable? |
| 14 | Transition support | If it automates or disrupts work, does it include retraining, income protections, worker voice, and realistic adaptation paths? |
| 15 | Economic justice | Does it measure success beyond productivity, growth, or revenue, including dignity, participation, equity, and social health? |
| 16 | Participation | Were affected communities involved before decisions were locked in? |
| 17 | Subsidiarity | Are decisions made at the most local, competent level possible, rather than imposed by distant technical or corporate power? |
| 18 | Pluralism and dialogue | Does it welcome diverse cultures, perspectives, and needs without forcing homogenization? |
| 19 | Non-neutrality awareness | Does the project acknowledge that tools encode values, incentives, assumptions, and power? |
| 20 | Human oversight | Are high-stakes decisions meaningfully reviewable by responsible humans? |
| 21 | Proportionality | Is the technology necessary and appropriate, or is it a technical answer looking for a human problem? |
| 22 | Nonviolence and peace | Does it avoid enabling war, repression, targeting, escalation, or dehumanization? |
| 23 | Misuse resistance | Has the project seriously anticipated abuse, fraud, exploitation, trafficking, manipulation, cyberattack, or authoritarian use? |
| 24 | Protection of civilians and the vulnerable | In high-risk contexts, does it prioritize those most likely to suffer from mistakes or misuse? |
| 25 | Environmental stewardship | Does it account for energy use, waste, extraction, water, emissions, and ecological cost? |
| 26 | Supply chain justice | Does it examine hidden labor, minerals, data work, moderation work, and outsourced harms behind the product? |
| 27 | Long-term stewardship | Does it plan for maintenance, dependency, obsolescence, repair, and downstream effects? |
| 28 | Humility | Does it avoid utopian promises, inevitability narratives, and move-fast arrogance? |
| 29 | Cooperation over domination | Does it build trust, mutual care, cooperation, and solidarity, or does it centralize power? |
| 30 | Hope with responsibility | Does it help people act constructively in the present rather than merely fear, wait, consume, or comment? |

## Minimum evidence checklist

- Written purpose statement naming the human good served.
- Map of affected communities, including those most likely to be harmed or excluded.
- Human rights, dignity, or social impact assessment.
- Evidence that affected people participated before major decisions were made.
- Clear accountability owners and appeal or remedy paths.
- Transparency about data, models, optimization goals, limitations, and known risks.
- Worker impact analysis, including automation, surveillance, wages, retraining, and hidden labor.
- Environmental accounting for energy, water, materials, emissions, waste, and infrastructure.
- Abuse and misuse threat modeling, including fraud, manipulation, trafficking, repression, cyberattack, and military use.
- Education, digital literacy, and responsible-use plan.
- Long-term stewardship plan for maintenance, access, portability, repair, and exit.

## Deal-breaker flags

- Treats people primarily as data, profiles, targets, users, consumers, risks, or units of productivity.
- Makes high-stakes decisions without meaningful human responsibility, appeal, or remedy.
- Concentrates essential data, infrastructure, or decision power in a few opaque hands.
- Depends on hidden exploitation in labor, resource extraction, moderation, data labeling, or supply chains.
- Claims moral neutrality while embedding contested assumptions in design, ranking, access, or enforcement.
- Weakens truth, trust, democratic deliberation, or the ability to distinguish fact from fiction.
- Exploits attention, vulnerability, loneliness, addiction, fear, outrage, or sexuality.
- Normalizes violence, repression, targeting, escalation, or removal of human judgment from lethal decisions.
- Promises inevitable progress while ignoring those who are displaced, excluded, surveilled, manipulated, or harmed.
