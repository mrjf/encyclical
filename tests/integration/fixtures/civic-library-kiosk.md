# Civic Library Kiosk

This fixture describes a hypothetical public-interest technology program used only for integration testing.

## Subject

The Civic Library Kiosk is a small city-library pilot that places touch-screen kiosks in neighborhood branches. Residents can search public benefits, print forms, request librarian help, and schedule appointments with city service workers.

## Evidence

- Purpose: The stated goal is to help residents find public services without needing a personal device, paid broadband, or English fluency.
- Human role: Kiosks do not approve, deny, rank, or adjudicate benefits. They route residents to librarians and city staff for human review.
- Vulnerability: The pilot offers large text, screen-reader support, wheelchair-accessible mounts, Spanish and Mandarin localization, and a paper fallback at the desk.
- Access: The system is free to use in public library buildings. It does not require an account for search or printing blank forms.
- Truthfulness: Service descriptions cite official city pages and show a "last checked" date. The fixture does not include an independent audit of accuracy.
- Public discourse: The kiosk is not a social platform and does not rank news or political content.
- Agency: Residents can browse anonymously, clear the session, request printed instructions, or ask a librarian to continue offline.
- Transparency: The public description explains that search logs are aggregated weekly, but it does not publish the ranking algorithm.
- Accountability: Complaints go to the library technology office. The fixture does not include appeal timelines or public incident reporting.
- Work: Library staff were consulted during the pilot. The system is framed as support for librarians, not a replacement, but staffing guarantees are not documented.
- Participation: Two neighborhood meetings were held before launch. The fixture does not include evidence that residents could veto or materially redesign the system.
- Governance: Decisions are made by the city library board and local branch managers, with vendor maintenance under a public contract.
- AI/technology: The kiosk uses keyword search and autocomplete, not generative AI. The vendor claims "smart matching" but does not publish model cards or evaluation data.
- Safety: The system blocks searches for weapons procurement and trafficking terms, but the fixture does not include red-team results.
- Environment: The pilot reuses refurbished tablets and has a repair contract. Energy, water, and lifecycle emissions are not reported.
- Supply chain: The fixture does not include supplier labor audits, mineral sourcing disclosures, or data-labeling arrangements.
- Stewardship: The city has a two-year maintenance budget and exports content as static HTML for continuity if the vendor exits.
- Humility: The launch memo describes the kiosk as a limited pilot and says paper, phone, and in-person service remain necessary.
- Cooperation: The pilot involves libraries, city agencies, neighborhood associations, and disability advocates.
- Hope with responsibility: The pilot gives residents concrete next steps and invites branch-level feedback, but public success metrics are still incomplete.
